Vibe: how my mom helped me create a new product

The result

What is it?

There is a company called Selerant that has an ecosystem of b2b products. These products help food companies manage the life cycle of their products from formulation to logistics and processing. The system is used by such companies as Nestle, Lindt, Mars Wrigley, Barilla, Bacardi, and Avon.

Task

To create Vibe, a tool for automating business processes in an existing b2b product ecosystem that:

  • is no code tool;
  • supports all existing objects from our various b2b systems;
  • interacts with third-party tools (for example, messengers);
  • scalable.

Research

Stakeholder interviews

We interviewed the main stakeholders to gather as much information about the product and business. This way we understood the goals of the product, where the money is, what they expect from our work, how they will evaluate success, what they want to achieve in the future and why, and most importantly, we synchronized our expectations.

Analyzing similar solutions

To avoid building a super-complex solution on our own, we first decided to look at others. Among them were Microsoft Power Automate, Salesforce Flow, Bizaggi, and Kissflow. Microsoft Power Automate turned out to be the most popular. It is not only intuitive and understandable, but also fit into our vision of the future product.

Microsoft Power Automate

Old solution and OOP

We found out from stakeholders that another team had once tried to do something similar, but it was shelved, and we should start with this. The solution was not very good - it had bad UX, looked like it came from the 90s, and even its creators could not even its creators couldn't figure out how it worked. However, it had an important element - it already contained all the objects with their properties that exist in our product ecosystem and the basic operations with them.

So we analyzed it, made an object model, and used it as a starting point for a new product.

How my mom helped

While I was making decisions in my head, I wasn't sure about the basic concept of what it should look like.

Once I was visiting my parents and returned home very late. And then my iPhone sent me a reminder to text my mom that I was home. Of course! My mom often worries, and I'm afraid of forgetting, so I automated it: I set it up in Shortcuts, that when I came home late (geolocation + time), my iPhone would suggest me to text my mom (just suggest, I don't need it to send a message every time ðŸ¤­). I remembered this at the right time, because this card interface fit perfectly into our idea. The principle of "not inventing your own, but reusing the existing" has once again saved my life.

Solution

I'll explain in very simple terms, but in reality, this is a product for a narrow circle of specialists with its own terminology.

The board and cards

Even at the initial stages, I realized that it would be a board with cards on it, but only now it began to make real sense and become concrete.

So, we have 3 main types of cards:

  1. Trigger. This is when the automatic flow starts. For example, when someone creates a new project.
  2. Action. This is what exactly will happen. For example, create a task, send an email, send a confirmation request to a manager, etc. Actions can take place linearly one after the other, or in parallel.
  3. Condition. This is a boolean expression, yes/no. We set a condition, if yes, then one action is performed, if no, then another.

Variables

But what if we want to access some property of an object to change it, insert a value, or check it? For this, we have variables, and that's what the object model was created for.

For example, when someone creates a new project, we want an email to be sent to the manager with the project name. With variables, we can insert the "project name" variable into another card and it will be populated where it needs to be every time. And all these works between our different products.

Integrations

So far, we've only done things inside our products, but what if we want to go outside? For example, send a message to Teams or Slack. For this, we will have API integrations. For users, it will look like just another card.

User testing

We created a clickable prototype to test the ideas on users. Luckily, we didn't have to look for them for a long time, because they were already existing users of our products. We found places where users did not understand or found it difficult, so the prototype went through several iterations. Mostly, these were fixes to micro-interactions.

Conclusions

So, my manager and I created and tested a new product that met the requirements:

  • is a no code tool;
  • can work with all products of our ecosystem;
  • has integrations that organically fit into the form of cards;
  • scalable, you can add new types of cards, objects, and integrations.

This was only the discovery phase, and we have a delivery phase coming up:

  • development, for which we have already prepared: at the previous stages, we consulted with developers and validated the solution for feasibility, and the design system on the design side is already ready;
  • product launch;
  • an endless cycle of feedback and iteration, because the product is never ready.
